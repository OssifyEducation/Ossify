What is the MSRA?
What is the MSRA and who needs to take it?
The Multi-Specialty Recruitment Assessment (MSRA) is a computer-based test used in the selection process for a number of NHS medical specialty training programmes. It assesses the essential competences set out in the national person specification through clinical scenarios.
Specialties currently using the MSRA include General Practice, Core Psychiatry, Clinical Radiology, Emergency Medicine, and others. Check your target specialty's recruitment page to confirm whether the MSRA applies to you.
How long is the MSRA?
The MSRA is 170 minutes in total and consists of two independently timed papers:
  • Professional Dilemmas (PD): 95 minutes (122 minutes with 25% extra time / 146 minutes with 50% extra time)
  • Clinical Problem Solving (CPS): 75 minutes (93 minutes with 25% extra time / 109 minutes with 50% extra time)
There is an optional 5-minute scheduled break between the two papers. Exceeding this break time automatically reduces the time available for the CPS paper.
What are the two papers in the MSRA?
The MSRA has two components:
  • Professional Dilemmas (PD) Paper
    A Situational Judgement Test (SJT) with 50 questions. You assume the role of an F2 doctor and respond to workplace dilemmas. Questions assess three core professional competencies: Commitment to professionalism, Coping with pressure, and Working effectively in a team.
    Roughly half the questions are ranking tasks (rank 4–5 actions from most to least appropriate) and half are multiple-choice. There is no negative marking.
  • Clinical Problem Solving (CPS) Paper
    Tests your ability to apply clinical knowledge and make clinical decisions in everyday practice. Questions are based on Foundation-level (FY2) clinical scenarios covering 12 topic areas. Contains 86 items split between Extended Matching Questions (EMQs) and Single Best Answers (SBAs). There is no negative marking.
The Clinical Problem Solving (CPS) Paper
What does the CPS paper test?
The CPS paper measures your ability to apply clinical knowledge and make clinical decisions in everyday practice. Importantly, it tests your ability to synthesise and apply medical knowledge — not just your ability to recall facts. All scenarios are based on Foundation (FY2) level clinical practice.
What clinical topics are covered in the CPS paper?
Each CPS test paper includes a balanced mix of scenarios across all 12 clinical topic areas:
  • Cardiovascular
  • Dermatology / ENT / Eyes
  • Endocrinology / Metabolic
  • Gastroenterology / Nutrition
  • Infectious disease / Haematology / Immunology / Allergies / Genetics
  • Musculoskeletal
  • Paediatrics
  • Pharmacology and therapeutics
  • Psychiatry / Neurology
  • Renal / Urology
  • Reproductive
  • Respiratory
What competency domains does the CPS paper assess?
The CPS paper covers 5 core domains:
  • Investigation — Specific investigations carried out to obtain a more conclusive diagnosis or rule out possible causes.
  • Diagnosis — Identification of a condition, disease or injury by evaluating the symptoms and signs presented by a patient.
  • Emergency — Recognition of potential serious illness requiring an immediate response.
  • Prescribing — Advising, authorising or reviewing the use of medicine or drugs as part of a patient's clinical management plan.
  • Management (non-prescribing) — Advising, authorising or reviewing non-medicinal forms of treatment.
What question formats appear in the CPS paper?
There are 86 items in the CPS paper. Around half are Extended Matching Questions (EMQs) and the other half are Single Best Answers (SBAs).
Extended Matching Questions (EMQs): You are presented with a list of 7 to 10 plausible response options, followed by multiple separate clinical scenarios all linked to that same response set. For each scenario you select the single most appropriate answer. Each response option can be selected once, more than once, or not at all.
Single Best Answers (SBAs): You are given one clinical scenario and asked to select the single most appropriate answer from 5 to 8 plausible response options.
Is there negative marking in the CPS paper?
No. One mark is awarded for each correct answer. There is no negative marking, so all questions should be attempted.
How are CPS questions developed and quality-assured?
CPS items are written by a well-established team of trained clinician item writers who develop new content annually. New items go through a rigorous multi-stage review process:
  • Initial review by a nominated clinician
  • Further review and refinement by subject matter expert (SME) writers
  • Review for fairness, language accessibility (English Not First Language review), and absence of locality-specific knowledge
  • Piloting alongside the live operational test
  • Psychometric review by a statistician examining item difficulty, candidate differentiation, and differential item functioning
Only items that perform satisfactorily across all these checks enter the operational item bank.
Booking & Registration
How do I register and book the MSRA?
You need a Pearson VUE account to book the MSRA. After applying through Oriel (oriel.nhs.uk), eligible applicants receive an invitation to book via their Pearson VUE account. Book as early as possible — popular test centre slots fill up quickly.
From 2025, applicants can submit to a maximum of 5 different specialties.
When is the MSRA held?
For Round 1 (2026 recruitment), two sittings are planned due to an anticipated rise in applicant numbers:
  • January 2026: Open to applicants for all specialties
  • February 2026: Reserved for Core Psychiatry and/or General Practice applicants only
If GP/Psychiatry applicant numbers exceed February capacity, some will be allocated to the January sitting. Confirmed sitting allocations are communicated after the application closing date.
Can I reschedule or cancel my booking?
Yes. Rescheduling and cancellations are managed through your Pearson VUE account. Check the NHS England MSRA pages for the specific deadlines that apply to your sitting, as late cancellations or missed tests may affect your application.
On the Day
What should I bring and what is not allowed?
You must bring: One original, valid, government-issued photo ID whose name matches your Pearson VUE account exactly. Expired IDs are accepted only with valid renewal documentation.
  • Not permitted in the testing room: Mobile phones, bags, notes, watches, or any personal items. Secure storage is typically available at the centre.
  • Permitted: A clear glass of water. Eating, smoking, and chewing gum are prohibited.
Arrive at least 15 minutes before your appointment. Late arrivals will not be admitted. Children are not allowed in assessment centres.
What happens on the day at the test centre?
On arrival you sign in and present your photo ID. Personal belongings are stored securely. You are escorted to a computer desk equipped with over-ear headphones (which can help block background noise). You will be given a whiteboard and pen for working — no paper is provided.
After 95 minutes, the PD paper ends and you receive an on-screen prompt offering the optional 5-minute break. If you take it, raise your hand so an invigilator can pause the test and let you out. If you exceed 5 minutes, the CPS paper timer starts automatically and the excess time is deducted.
Is remote testing available?
Remote testing via Pearson VUE's OnVUE platform is available in limited circumstances. Eligibility criteria apply and must be arranged through your Pearson VUE account. For OnVUE tests, any unauthorised break automatically ends your assessment with no re-sit option.
Scores & Outcomes
How is the MSRA scored?
Scores for each paper are normalised around a mean of 250 with a standard deviation of 40. Because of this normalisation, there is no fixed maximum score — your result is relative to the performance of your cohort.
Scores are also placed into bands 1 to 4. Achieving a band of 1 in either paper means you have not met the minimum acceptable standard and your application will not progress further.
The weighting given to each paper differs by specialty. For example: General Practice and Core Psychiatry weight the PD paper more heavily. Emergency Medicine weights PD and CPS equally.
What score do I need to pass?
There is no single universal pass mark. Scoring thresholds and band definitions vary by specialty. For Core Psychiatry, a minimum raw score of 186 in each paper is required to be considered appointable. For GP, band 1 in either paper means automatic non-progression. Always check your target specialty's recruitment page for the current threshold.
Can I appeal my MSRA result?
No. Your MSRA outcome is final and cannot be reviewed, re-marked, or disputed. There is no appeals process.
Can I transfer a previous MSRA score to a new application?
This depends on the specialty:
  • Core Psychiatry: Score transfer is available within a 12-month recruitment cycle, provided the recruitment process is unchanged. You must opt in at the point of application — this cannot be changed after submission. If you transfer, your rank may go up or down depending on the new cohort's performance.
  • General Practice (Round 1 / August intake): Score transfer is not available — you must sit the MSRA in that round's window. If you re-sit, the re-sit outcome supersedes your original score; you cannot revert to a higher earlier score. The decision to re-sit must be made at the point of application and cannot be changed afterwards.
Preparation
Is there an official practice paper?
Yes. NHS England publishes an official MSRA practice paper with an answer key and rationales, available on the NHS Medical Hub publications page. This is the best starting point for understanding the question format and standard expected.
Pearson VUE also offers a generic test-taking demo to help you familiarise yourself with the on-screen controls and layout before your test.
What is the best way to prepare for the PD paper?
The PD paper is an aptitude test, not a knowledge test. Preparation should focus on the three core competency areas: Commitment to professionalism, Coping with pressure, and Effective teamwork.
Align your thinking with GMC guidance and NHS England frameworks — question writers work from these official sources. Practise SJT-style ranking and multiple-choice questions to build exam technique. A key point: ranking answers treat each option as an independent, discrete action — not as steps in a chronological sequence.
What is the best way to prepare for the CPS paper?
The CPS paper tests applied clinical knowledge, not rote recall. Focus on understanding and synthesising clinical presentations across all 12 topic areas rather than memorising facts in isolation.
  • Timed question practice (EMQ and SBA formats)
  • Reviewing detailed answer rationales to understand clinical reasoning
  • Practising time management (75 minutes for 86 questions)
  • Prioritising the 5 core domains: Investigation, Diagnosis, Emergency, Prescribing, and Management
  • Targeting high-yield topics relevant to your specialty if aiming to maximise domain-specific performance
Using a question bank that mirrors the EMQ and SBA formats — and covers all 12 clinical topic areas — is one of the most effective preparation strategies.
Are there accommodations for candidates with disabilities?
Yes. Additional time is available in line with the Equality Act 2010 disability accommodation requirements. Standard adjustments are:
  • +25%: PD 122 minutes / CPS 93 minutes
  • +50%: PD 146 minutes / CPS 109 minutes
Contact the MSRA team at england.msra@nhs.net to discuss your requirements as early as possible.
