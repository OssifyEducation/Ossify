Evidence-based preparation

Keys to Success.

How to get the most out of Ossify — and out of yourself.
Passing the MSRA is not just about how many questions you do. It is about how you revise, how you rest, how you process information, and how you manage the pressure of high-stakes preparation. The following principles are drawn from cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and the science of expert performance — applied directly to MSRA preparation.

1
Retrieval Practice
Test yourself. Don't just read.
The science

Decades of research in cognitive psychology — most famously the work of Henry Roediger and colleagues — demonstrate that actively retrieving information from memory is significantly more effective at building long-term knowledge than passively re-reading or highlighting notes. This is known as the Testing Effect or Retrieval Practice Effect.

What this means for your MSRA prep

Reading through guidelines or lecture notes feels productive but creates an illusion of learning. Your brain mistakes familiarity for genuine understanding. When you are tested on that same material — when you have to actively pull the answer out — your brain encodes it more deeply and durably.

How to use Ossify for this

Do not read first, then test. Test first. Attempt questions on a topic before you feel fully ready. The act of struggling to retrieve information — even unsuccessfully — primes your brain to encode the correct answer when you see the explanation. This is called "desirable difficulty" and it is one of the most powerful tools in learning science.

Quick tip

After reading an explanation, close it and try to recall the three key teaching points from memory. This single step dramatically improves long-term retention compared to reading alone.

2
Spaced Repetition
Review at the right time, not the most time.
The science

Hermann Ebbinghaus's Forgetting Curve — first described in 1885 and repeatedly confirmed since — shows that memory decays predictably over time unless we revisit material at strategic intervals. Spaced repetition exploits this curve by scheduling reviews just before you are about to forget, which pushes the memory further into long-term storage with each successful recall.

What this means for your MSRA prep

Cramming a topic intensively for one session and never revisiting it is one of the least effective revision strategies. You may score well on a question block the same day, but the knowledge will decay rapidly if not reinforced. Spreading your revision — returning to material across days and weeks — produces far more durable learning for the same time investment.

How to use Ossify for this

Use the Revision Mode to regularly revisit questions you have flagged or previously answered incorrectly. Do not just re-do them once and consider them done. Space your return visits. Topics should be revisited at increasing intervals: review after one day, then three days, then a week, then two weeks. Each successful recall strengthens the memory further.

Quick tip

Set aside the first 15 minutes of each revision session to review yesterday's flagged questions before moving on to new material. This single habit can dramatically improve retention across your preparation period.

3
Interleaving
Mix it up — even when it feels harder.
The science

Research in educational psychology — including landmark work by Robert Bjork at UCLA — shows that mixing different topics or question types within a single revision session (interleaving) produces better long-term retention and transfer of knowledge than blocking practice on a single topic until it feels mastered (blocking). Blocked practice feels better in the moment. Interleaved practice produces better outcomes. This gap between perceived difficulty and actual learning is called the "desirable difficulty" phenomenon.

What this means for your MSRA prep

The MSRA Clinical Problem Solving paper tests multiple specialties across a single sitting. If you revise only cardiology for three weeks, then only respiratory, then only gastroenterology, you may feel confident in each topic — but your ability to switch rapidly between them under pressure will be underdeveloped.

How to use Ossify for this

After you have a baseline understanding of individual topics, begin mixing your question blocks. Combine Clinical Problem Solving questions from multiple specialties within a single session. Alternate between CPS and Professional Dilemmas questions. This feels harder — and that difficulty is the signal that deeper learning is happening.

Quick tip

Once per week, run a fully randomised, mixed-topic block on Ossify. Treat the feeling of uncertainty and difficulty as a positive signal, not a sign you are not ready.

4
Elaborative Interrogation
Ask why, not just what.
The science

Elaborative interrogation is a technique from cognitive psychology that involves asking "why" and "how" questions about the material you are learning, rather than simply accepting facts. Research shows this dramatically improves comprehension and retention because it forces you to connect new information to existing knowledge frameworks — creating a richer, more retrievable memory.

What this means for your MSRA prep

It is not enough to know that the answer is "refer to a senior colleague" in a Professional Dilemmas question. The questions in the MSRA are constructed to test your understanding of the principles underpinning that answer — patient safety, GMC guidance, professional relationships, transparency. If you understand the why, you can apply the principle to novel scenarios you have never seen before.

How to use Ossify for this

When you read an explanation, do not stop when you understand which answer is correct. Ask yourself: "Why does this principle exist?" "What would happen if the opposite were true?" "How does this connect to what I know about GMC Good Medical Practice?" This active interrogation transforms surface-level answer review into genuine conceptual learning.

Quick tip

For every Professional Dilemma question you get wrong, write one sentence explaining the underlying principle being tested — not the answer, the principle. After two weeks of this habit, your reasoning on novel SJT scenarios will be noticeably sharper.

5
Deliberate Practice
Progress is a skill — and neuroplasticity makes it possible.
The science

Neuroplasticity — the brain's lifelong ability to form new connections and reorganise itself in response to learning — is most efficiently activated through deliberate practice: focused, effortful engagement with material at the edge of your current competence. Research by Anders Ericsson, whose work underpins the "10,000 hour" concept, shows that it is not raw volume of practice that drives expert performance — it is the quality of attention brought to the practice.

What this means for your MSRA prep

Doing 100 questions on autopilot in a state of half-distraction produces far less learning than doing 40 questions with full attention, active reasoning, and engaged review of every explanation. The brain builds new pathways through effortful, focused engagement — not passive volume.

How to use Ossify for this

Set yourself focused revision blocks of no longer than 45–60 minutes at a time. Before you start each block, set a specific intention: "In this session I am going to work on my reasoning for acute presentations" or "Today I will focus on rank-ordering Professional Dilemma questions where patient safety is competing with team relationships." Focused intent before a session dramatically improves what you absorb during it.

Quick tip

The Pomodoro Technique — 25 minutes of concentrated work, 5 minute break, repeat — has strong empirical support for maintaining high-quality attention. Try applying it to your Ossify sessions rather than long, unbroken revision marathons.

6
Sleep & Consolidation
Rest is revision.
The science

Memory consolidation — the process by which newly learned information is transferred into long-term storage — happens primarily during sleep, particularly during slow-wave and REM stages. Research has demonstrated that sleep deprivation dramatically impairs both the encoding of new information and the consolidation of recently learned material. A night of good sleep after a revision session is not a break from learning. It is a critical part of the learning cycle.

What this means for your MSRA prep

The week of all-nighters before your exam is not just unpleasant — it is counter-productive. Information encoded while sleep-deprived is poorly consolidated. Questions completed in a state of fatigue produce less durable learning. And sleep deprivation on exam day meaningfully impairs reasoning and decision-making — the exact skills the MSRA tests.

Quick tip

Review your most important or most recently struggled-with topics in the 30 minutes before sleep. Research suggests that material reviewed immediately before sleep benefits from enhanced consolidation during the night that follows.

7
Mindset & Stress Regulation
Manage the pressure.
The science

Research in performance psychology — including Carol Dweck's work on Growth Mindset — demonstrates that candidates who view ability as developable respond more effectively to failure and difficult material than those who treat performance as a fixed reflection of intelligence. Additionally, moderate stress enhances performance through cortisol and noradrenaline — the Yerkes-Dodson curve — but high chronic stress impairs the prefrontal cortex function needed for clinical reasoning and decision-making.

What this means for your MSRA prep

Getting questions wrong is not a sign you are not cut out for your specialty. It is an essential part of the learning process. Every error on Ossify is diagnostic information about a gap in your understanding — information you now have the opportunity to address. Approach each wrong answer with curiosity rather than self-criticism.

Practical strategies
  • Brief daily mindfulness or breathing practice — even five minutes of diaphragmatic breathing reduces cortisol measurably.
  • Maintain physical exercise throughout your preparation period. The evidence for its positive effects on memory consolidation and anxiety reduction is robust.
  • Maintain social connection. Isolation during revision is a risk factor for anxiety, not a sign of commitment.
  • Reframe exam anxiety as arousal. Research shows that reappraising anxiety as excitement — rather than trying to suppress it — improves performance on cognitively demanding tasks.
Quick tip

Before your exam, try the simple reframe: "I am excited." It sounds trivial. The evidence behind it is not.

8
Consistency over Intensity
Build the habit.
The science

Behavioural science research on habit formation shows that consistent daily behaviour is far more effective for long-term outcomes than occasional high-intensity effort. Consistency also reduces the cognitive overhead of deciding when and whether to revise, which is itself a drain on willpower and motivation.

What this means for your MSRA prep

Thirty focused minutes on Ossify every day is more valuable than an occasional four-hour session once a week. Daily engagement keeps the material active in your memory, builds revision as a habitual part of your routine, and distributes your learning across time in a way that maximises spaced repetition benefits.

How to build the habit

Choose a fixed time for your daily Ossify session and anchor it to an existing routine — immediately after morning coffee, on your commute, in your lunch break. Do not rely on motivation to begin. Rely on the system.

Quick tip

Set a daily minimum that feels almost embarrassingly achievable — even ten questions counts. The goal is to protect the streak. On hard days, hitting the minimum keeps momentum. On good days, you will naturally do more.
